Project 03 / 04
Penn GSE · Curriculum Design · Higher Education
Stress Workshop for College Students
A 3-module in-person well-being workshop designed for early-year undergraduates helping them recognize stress, understand its mechanisms, and build a personal coping toolkit. Grounded in learner-centered ideology and spiral curriculum structure.
Penn GSE Coursework Curriculum Design Project In-person Workshop Undergraduate Learners

Overarching Goal

Support students in developing the ability to manage stress through self-regulation and seeking appropriate support not just for now, but across future academic and personal challenges.

Frameworks Applied

  • Backward Design (Desired Results → Evidence → Learning Plan)
  • Spiral curriculum structure
  • Learner-centered ideology
  • Bloom's Taxonomy for objective alignment

Learners

Early-year undergraduate students navigating the transition from high school to university facing increased academic demands, social changes, and new living environments.

The Problem This Addresses

A gap in the null curriculum

Universities offer many mental health resources but rarely teach students how to recognize stress in the first place. Students often misread chronic stress as simply being "tired" or "busy," and only seek help once stress has escalated into depression or crisis. This workshop addresses what is not explicitly taught: the ability to identify stress early, understand what drives it, and act before it compounds.

Rather than a psychology lecture, this curriculum functions as a facilitated well-being workshop interactive, personal, and grounded in self-reflection and experiential learning.

Curriculum Structure

Three modules, spiral design

The course follows a spiral structure with a "notice → understand → apply" cycle. Each module revisits students' own stress experiences at increasing depth:

Module 1
Recognizing Stress
Self-diagnosis & Triggers
"How do I know when I am under stress, and what might be causing it?"
Module 2
Understanding Stress
Biology, psychology & behavior
"Why does understanding stress matter for me?"
Module 3
Managing Stress
Strategies & support
"Which strategies may actually work for me, and how do I know when to reach out?"

The spiral logic: students first notice their own stress signs (Module 1), then understand the biological and psychological mechanisms behind what they noticed (Module 2), then apply coping strategies to their own concrete experiences (Module 3). Each loop deepens comprehension by returning to the same personal examples with new conceptual tools.

Module 1 Deep Dive Desired Results & Assessment

From learning goals to evidence

Backward Design drove the full module planning. Here is how Module 1's goals map to evidence:

Goal Type Goal Evidence / Assessment
Meaning Stress shows up in body, emotions, thoughts, and behavior noticing it early makes management possible Self-reflection chart: identify ≥1 signal per category + explain why early recognition matters
Meaning Triggers are personal and contextual; patterns can be identified and changed Pair activity: share 1–2 stressful situations; explain how they responded across body, thoughts, emotions
Acquisition Define and describe common stress triggers: academic pressure, social adjustment, homesickness Mini poster: illustrate ≥3 college stress triggers with definitions and examples
Transfer Use self-awareness strategies to recognize stress during transitionary moments in college and beyond Written reflection: describe a recent stressful situation and the self-awareness strategy used
Activity Design Module 1

Four activities, prerequisite-linear sequence

Module 1 progresses from simple to complex: recognizing signals → identifying triggers → understanding patterns → integrating all three through self-diagnosis.

Activity 1
What counts as emotional signals?
Pairs discuss what they think counts as emotional stress signals, then compare their answers to a chart of common signs identifying what they knew and what surprised them.
Activity 2
Triggers self-check
Students write down two recent stressful situations, then categorize each using a trigger chart (academic, social, financial, family) and reflect on which are most common in their own lives.
Activity 3
Which pattern fits?
Students match real college-life scenarios to stress patterns (acute, chronic, transitional), then reflect on which pattern most closely describes their own recent experience.
Activity 4
Reflection & self-diagnosis
Students integrate signs, triggers, and patterns by writing a short reflection on a recent stressful situation applying the self-awareness strategies from across the module.
Design Rationale & Reflections

What shaped the key design decisions

  • Why learner-centered? Stress recognition is inherently personal. A teacher-centered approach would impose a generic framework onto experiences that are deeply individual. Students needed space to surface their own patterns before any abstract content could land.
  • Why spiral structure? Starting with personal experience gives students a concrete anchor. Returning to those same experiences in Modules 2 and 3 with new conceptual tools biological mechanisms, then coping strategies means the learning compounds rather than accumulates.
  • Why address the null curriculum? The absence of explicit stress literacy in higher education is itself a design choice one with consequences. This workshop treats well-being knowledge as a learnable skill, not a personality trait or a crisis response.
  • Assessment rationale: All assessments are performance-based self-reflection charts, pair sharing, mini posters, written reflections. None are tests of memorization. The goal is for students to use the knowledge about their own lives, not recite it back.
Project 04 / 04
UCL · EdTech Research · Qualitative Study
Rethinking Web 2.0 in Higher Education: Balancing Affordances, Pedagogy, and Student Agency
A qualitative study examining how university students actually experience using Padlet and what happens when instructional design doesn't account for student agency.
University College London BA Dissertation Qualitative Research Higher Education Web 2.0 / EdTech

Research Question

How does the use of Padlet in UK university classrooms affect students' learning experiences?

Methods

  • Semi-structured interviews with 4 undergraduate students
  • Thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006)
  • Emotional valence coding (positive / negative / neutral)
  • Cross-coded by interaction type → co-occurrence matrix

Theoretical Framework

  • Actor-Network Theory (Latour, 2005)
  • Selwyn's critical EdTech perspective
Why This Study

The gap in EdTech research

Most research on Web 2.0 tools like Padlet, Google Docs, and Mentimeter focuses on their benefits increased participation, collaborative features, and engagement. But this celebratory narrative misses something important: what actually happens for students when these tools are used poorly, or without attention to their needs?

This study was motivated by a simple observation: tools that look great in theory can frustrate students in practice. The aim was to examine both sides affordances and drawbacks grounded in students' real classroom experiences at a UK university.

Key Findings

What the data revealed

✦ What Worked
  • → Anonymity increased participation comfort students felt safe expressing themselves without fear of judgment
  • → Peer comments and public interaction generated mostly positive emotional responses
  • → Multimedia sharing and asynchronous posting gave students flexible ways to contribute
✦ Where It Broke Down
  • → Extended Padlet use during seminars replaced verbal discussion, frustrating students who expected face-to-face interaction
  • → Compulsory weekly posts created stress and reduced student agency
  • → Delayed feedback from instructors generated negative emotional responses

The central tension: teachers' positive outcome beliefs about Padlet led them to over-rely on it, without sufficiently accounting for students' desire for authentic, discussion-based learning. According to Actor-Network Theory, outcomes depend not just on the tool's features, but on how students, instructors, and the platform interact together.

Implications for ID Practice

What this means for instructional designers

This research shaped how I think about technology integration decisions in my own design work. Three takeaways I carry into every project:

  • Affordances ≠ outcomes. A tool's features only matter if the instructional design creates space for students to use them in ways that feel meaningful. Anonymity is powerful but only if students have something worth saying anonymously.
  • Student agency is a design variable. Mandatory participation structures can undermine the very engagement they're trying to create. Designing for optional, flexible contribution often yields more authentic participation than compulsory tasks.
  • Technology should complement, not replace, interaction. When Padlet dominated seminar time instead of supplementing discussion, students felt cheated of the face-to-face connection they came for. The best uses of EdTech expand what's possible they don't substitute for human interaction.
